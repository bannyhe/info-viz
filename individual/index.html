Introduction

The Nash Equilibrium, named after the late mathematician John Forbes Nash Jr., is a proposed solution of a non-cooperative game involving two or more players, each player is assumed to know the equilibrium strategies of the other players, while no one has anything to gain by changing only their own strategy. In game theory, multiple player has to make a decision to maximize their profit, if each player has chosen a strategy, and no player can benefit by changing strategies while the other players keep theirs unchanged, then the current set of strategy choices and their corresponding payoffs constitutes a Nash Equilibrium.

Traditionally, the most common solution in Nash Equilibrium is two pure-strategy Nash Equilibria: both choose the same thing to do. However, there is also a mixed strategy Nash Equilibrium. In order to achieve it, both players need to be indifferent between the two choices, and therefore willing to play a Mixed Strategy.

Typical Example

Suppose two students (A and B) need to study for math and physics exam under same limited time, in order to achieve a common goal: earning extra credit as much as possible, both players need to make decision between studying for math or physics (cooperate or not), and therefore brings about a mixed strategy. Expected Value

According to its definition, the expected value of a discrete random variable is the probability-weighted average of all possible values. In other words, each possible value the random variable can assume is multiplied by its probability of occurring, and the resulting products are summed to produce the expected value.

In finite case, let X be a random variable with a finite number of finite outcomes X1, X2, X3, ..., ...Xn occurring with probabilities with P1, P2, P3, ..., ..., pn, respectively. The expected value of X is defined as:

E(X) = X1P1 + X2P2 + X3P3 + ... + XnPn
